# No Single Substrate

**Summary**: Levin's hypothesis that there is no single substrate for memory. Synaptic machinery is for interpretation, not storage. Memory is distributed across bioelectric, biomechanical, and biochemical reservoirs at every scale of the body. This is the strongest substrate claim in the wiki.

**Sources**: Self-Improvising Memory.pdf, self-improvising-memories.pdf, Polycomputing - Room Right Here.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-07-10

---

## The claim, in Levin's own words

From the paper (source: Self-Improvising Memory.pdf): "My current hypothesis, driven by the above multi-scale perspective, is that there is no single substrate for memory. Every component of the system, including but not limited to those that bubble up to conscious recollections, could be using everything in its environment as an interpretable scratchpad."

From the blog comment thread (source: self-improvising-memories.pdf): "I think biology treats all the parts, including the bioelectric, biomechanical, and all the biochemical events, as a kind of reservoir (in the sense of reservoir computing). I suspect the synaptic machinery is mostly for interpretation of the memory, not storage."

This is the wiki's strongest substrate-independence claim.

## What this contradicts

- The textbook neuroscience view: episodic memory is stored in hippocampal-temporal networks, consolidated to cortex.
- The neural-network metaphor: memory equals weights in a connectionist net.
- The database metaphor in AI: memory equals records in a structured store.

In each of these, the substrate is specific. Levin's claim is that the **interpretation process** is what makes a substrate a memory substrate, and the same physical components can serve other roles depending on the polycomputing perspective ([polycomputing](/polycomputing)).

## Evidence Levin cites

- Animal memory transfer via brain extracts (Aplysia RNA experiments by Glanzman). The extract does not need careful placement; the recipient brain extracts meaning regardless.
- Memory transplant claims from heart and lung transplants (uncertain but suggestive).
- Memory persistence across caterpillar-to-butterfly metamorphosis ([mnemonic-improvisation](/mnemonic-improvisation)).
- Planarian regeneration of trained behaviour from tail fragments.
- Tadpoles with ectopic eyes (on their tails) learning to see, connecting to the spinal cord rather than the brain.
- The salamander tail-on-flank remodel, where cells gradually reinterpret their position.

In all these, "memory" persists across substrate changes that would destroy any single-substrate account.

## Reservoir computing

Levin connects the idea to **reservoir computing**: a class of machine-learning architectures that exploit the rich dynamics of a fixed, randomly connected reservoir as a feature space, training only the readout. The reservoir does not need to be carefully designed; its inherent dynamics are the substrate. The reading layer is the interpreter.

If biology runs on a similar principle, then the body itself is the reservoir, and synaptic networks are the trained readouts.

This is no longer an analogy resting on the memory paper alone. The polycomputing source ([source-bongard-levin-polycomputing](/source-bongard-levin-polycomputing)) reviews **physical reservoir computing** as an engineered reality: inert bulk materials (metal plates, photonic crystals) are excited and read out, and "deep physical neural networks" encode both input and weights as forces on an unchanged material, so one substrate embodies many computations. That is engineered substrate flexibility. See [engineered-substrate-flexibility](/engineered-substrate-flexibility).

## Why this matters

This is the most radical claim that the wiki rests on. If Levin is right:

- Synaptic-storage neuroscience captures the readout, not the substrate.
- Engineered AI memory should consider non-symbolic, dynamics-based substrates (reservoir computing, continuous-attractor networks, neuromorphic chips), not just database backends.
- The right question for AI agent memory is not "where do we store it" but "what dynamics can sustain agent-relevant reinterpretation across time?"

Flagged as **load-bearing and contested**. The conventional neuroscience view (hippocampal episodic storage) is well-evidenced for vertebrate memory. Levin acknowledges most of his evidence is from invertebrate models. The synthesis with Conway ([conway-2005-memory-and-self](/conway-2005-memory-and-self)) is at the level of function, not implementation. See [open-questions](/open-questions).
