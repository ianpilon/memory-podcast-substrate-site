# Clive Wearing

**Summary**: A British musicologist who suffered brain damage in 1985 and lost the ability to form new episodic memories. His memory resets approximately every 30 seconds. Two things survived: his procedural musical ability and his emotional bond with his wife. Used by Youssef as the anchor case showing what fails when episodic memory is destroyed but emotional and procedural memory remain.

**Sources**: Robert Youssef x post.pdf, Conway_2005.pdf (Wilson, Baddeley, Kapur 1995 case)

**Last updated**: 2026-05-27

---

## The case

In 1985, Clive Wearing contracted herpes simplex encephalitis, which destroyed his hippocampus and surrounding medial temporal lobe. Since then his episodic memory resets every ~30 seconds. He has written hundreds of diary entries that begin "Now I am truly awake for the first time," crosses them out as he sees the earlier ones, and writes the same sentence again minutes later (source: Robert Youssef x post.pdf).

Two capacities survived:

- **Procedural memory**: he can still play piano. Procedural skills are stored in cerebellum and basal ganglia, not in the damaged hippocampus.
- **Emotional memory**: every time his wife enters the room, he greets her with overwhelming joy, as if reunited after years. Every single time. Emotional memory is distributed widely (amygdala, cortex) and survives damage that obliterates episodic memory.

Conway also cites Wearing in his 2005 paper as evidence for episodic-memory loss producing extreme storage-cost arguments (Wearing initially kept several diaries because every few minutes he wrote that he had just awoken from being dead).

## What it shows

The episodic memory system can be destroyed entirely while procedural and emotional systems remain functional. Wearing can:

- Operate moment to moment (play music, recognize his wife, walk around).
- Maintain emotional bonds.
- Express preferences and personality.

He cannot:

- Form a continuous narrative of his life.
- Track goal progress beyond seconds.
- Build new self-knowledge.

In Conway's terms: the [autobiographical-knowledge-base](/autobiographical-knowledge-base) is intact for pre-injury life, but new [episodic-memory](/episodic-memory) cannot be formed and integrated. The [working-self](/working-self) cannot do its goal-tracking job. The [self-memory-system](/self-memory-system) is broken at its foundation.

## Why Youssef uses it

Wearing is Youssef's anchor for the claim that **episodic buffers are not enough**. Most current AI agent architectures have effectively Wearing-equivalent memory: 30 seconds (or 4k tokens, or 128k tokens) of context, with no integration into a coherent self-history. Long contexts extend the buffer but do not solve the problem.

The case also shows the **modular** nature of memory systems. Procedural is one system, emotional is another, episodic is a third. Each can fail independently. In AI agent design, this maps to keeping skills (in weights), conversational style (in system prompt), and episodes (in retrieval) as separable subsystems.

## Why Levin would highlight different features

Wearing's emotional memory is interesting in Levin's frame for a different reason: it is distributed across cortex, amygdala, and likely body-state systems. The case is implicit evidence for [no-single-substrate](/no-single-substrate). The emotional bond did not live in the hippocampus that got destroyed; it lived elsewhere, and "elsewhere" turns out to be many places at once.
