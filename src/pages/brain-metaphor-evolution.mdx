# Brain Metaphor Evolution

**Summary**: The Schleisman article's historical observation that each century has described the brain using the cutting-edge technology of its day. Hydraulics, electricity, telegraph wires, computers. The current "neural net" metaphor will likely be replaced, and confidence that LLMs are "brain-like" should be tempered accordingly.

**Sources**: The promise-of-agi-neuroscience.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-27

---

## The pattern

The article tracks the metaphor by century (source: The promise-of-agi-neuroscience.pdf):

- **17th century (Descartes)**: brain works like a hydraulic system. Pressure-driven fluid in nerve channels.
- **18th century (Galvani)**: brain works like electricity. Nerve impulses as electrical phenomena.
- **19th century (Helmholtz)**: brain works like telegraph wires. Signals transmitted over discrete channels with measurable speeds.
- **20th century (McCulloch and Pitts)**: brain works like a computer. The McCulloch-Pitts neuron is a binary threshold gate; neurons compute.

The pattern: the mind-brain is the most complex system humans know of, so we imagine it functioning like the most complex technology we have built. Each metaphor was useful for what it could capture and wrong for what it forced us to ignore.

## What this implies for "brains as neural nets"

If history is any guide, the current "brain works like a neural net" metaphor is also likely to be wrong. The Schleisman piece notes that future technology X may yet emerge, more complex than computation, and become the new metaphor.

The point is not that all metaphors are equally bad. Each has been a productive approximation. The point is that **confidence the current metaphor is the right one is unwarranted**. The 19th century telegraph theorists were also confident. So were the 17th century hydraulic theorists.

## What this implies for AGI claims

Two practical implications:

- The computational theory of mind ([computational-theory-of-mind-critique](/computational-theory-of-mind-critique)) may turn out to be false, in which case AGI cannot be reached by purely connectionist scaling.
- Even if computation is the right substrate, current neural net architectures may not be in the right computational class. The Schleisman piece lists five reasons LLMs are not brain-like.

The piece is careful not to overclaim. AGI **might** still arise from neural net architectures very unlike biological brains, just as airplanes fly without flapping. Automobile engineers did not model horse physiology. This is the philosophical principle of multiple realizability.

But: **if** AGI requires brain-like processing, then current architectures are missing a lot, and the missing pieces are at the [meso-level-theory-gap](/meso-level-theory-gap) that neither neuroscience nor cognitive science has yet bridged.

## Connection to the wiki's substrate question

This piece is the critique-side complement to the substrate-flexibility claim in [no-single-substrate](/no-single-substrate). Levin says biology runs memory on every substrate it can find. The Schleisman piece says we do not yet know which substrates can run cognition, and the current default (connectionist nets) may be insufficient even if computation is the right level of analysis.

Both undermine confidence in the textbook answer (brain = neural net, neural net implements memory, therefore memory = activations and weights). The substrate question is genuinely open.
