# PTSD and Memory Distortion

**Summary**: Conway's clinical evidence that the [working-self](/working-self) uses memory distortion to protect goal coherence under traumatic threat. Two case studies show how vivid intrusive memories can be coherence-protective (preserving the goal-system) at the cost of correspondence-accuracy, and how CBT works by reinstating correspondence.

**Sources**: Conway_2005.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-27

---

## Two cases

### Road Traffic Accident (RTA) patient

A professional driver was in a high-speed collision. The air bag deployment produced powder he thought was smoke; he could smell petrol; he thought "I will be burned alive" and that his wife had died. After the accident he developed intrusive flashbacks in which he believed he could have averted the crash by shouting a warning to the other driver. He experienced overwhelming guilt and self-blame.

In CBT, police records and his own memory established that there was in fact no possibility of him shouting a warning. In his memory, time had been dilated so it appeared he had the opportunity but had failed to act. As CBT continued, the time dilation gradually weakened, his memory came to accurately reflect the speed of the collision, and his intrusions and guilt diminished (source: Conway_2005.pdf).

### 9/11 witness

A middle-aged woman watched the planes hit the World Trade Center from a nearby street. She developed a distorted flashback: an "observer perspective" image of herself high above the ground watching the collision. The scene was peaceful with no noise. CBT included reinstating the field perspective and the sounds of the moment. PTSD symptoms then improved.

In her case the original memory was clearly false; she had been on the street, not above. But the distortion served a function: it kept her at affective distance from the experience and prevented engagement with the emotional content.

## What the cases show

Conway uses these to argue that memory distortion in PTSD is **functional** for the [working-self](/working-self):

- The RTA driver maintained a self-image as "professional, in control" by inventing a missed opportunity to warn. This preserved his identity at the cost of intense guilt.
- The 9/11 witness maintained affective distance by adopting an impossible observer perspective.

In both cases, the working self distorted memory to maintain coherence with self-images that the truth would threaten. CBT works in part by **reinstating correspondence**: forcing the memory to match the actual event, even though the self-image must then revise.

## The control-failure angle

In Conway's broader framework, frontal damage produces confabulation that is **uncontrolled** rather than functional. Patient AO (right-hemisphere damage) confabulated paranoid memories of being moved between rooms at night and being beaten by "evil nurses." These memories served her current goal-state (denial of her physical and mental incapacities) but were not produced by an intact, regulating working self.

So the SMS view is that:

- Healthy memory: correspondence-biased recent, coherence-biased long-term, balanced by [coherence-vs-correspondence](/coherence-vs-correspondence).
- PTSD: working self over-uses coherence-driven distortion to manage trauma. Reversible with therapy.
- Frontal confabulation: working-self regulation is impaired; coherence-driven distortion runs unchecked.

## Connection to Levin

Levin would argue that all three cases are showing the same underlying process: [confabulation-as-feature](/confabulation-as-feature). The clinical pathologies are not deviations from a faithful default; they are control failures of the universal active-reinterpretation engine.

This is the sharpest theoretical tension in the wiki ([confabulation-as-feature](/confabulation-as-feature) vs Conway's [coherence-vs-correspondence](/coherence-vs-correspondence)). Both could be right at different levels of analysis: Conway is describing the regulated vertebrate SMS, Levin is describing the cross-scale architecture beneath it.
