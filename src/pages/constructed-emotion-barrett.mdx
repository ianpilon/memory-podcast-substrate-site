# Constructed Emotion (Barrett)

**Summary**: Lisa Feldman Barrett's theory of constructed emotion, raised in the comment thread of [levin-2024-blog-supplement](/levin-2024-blog-supplement) by Benjamin L. Emotions are not pre-programmed reactions but **concepts** that win a competition among neural patterns to be the best available prediction for matching interoceptive data in service of allostasis.

**Sources**: self-improvising-memories.pdf (comment thread)

**Last updated**: 2026-05-27

---

## The theory

In Barrett's view, the brain runs an internal model that tries to make sense of interoceptive signals in light of sensory data (context) and previous experience. Rather than parsing every interoceptive signal individually (too demanding), the brain relies on **concepts** to make sense of what is happening (source: self-improvising-memories.pdf).

A concept is a collection of predictions in the form of competing neural patterns. Each pattern strives to be the best available prediction for matching the incoming data, where "best" means achieving allostasis (homeostatic balance), not objective accuracy. The winning prediction becomes the conscious experience. Emotions are a subset of concepts that categorize sensations to give them meaning.

These concepts are not given by a genetic emotion-code (the classical view, e.g. Ekman's basic emotions). They are constructed by the brain on the fly based on what was most useful in similar past circumstances.

## Why Levin found this useful

Levin replied to Benjamin L: "thanks, this is very useful! I hadn't been aware of the connection to her work. Will read up."

The reason the connection matters: Barrett's framework is a working theory of how [memories-as-agents](/memories-as-agents) could be implemented at the neural level. Competing predictions are exactly the "active patterns with minimal agency" Levin proposes. The winning concept is the metastable pattern that, in his words, "wants to construct the rest of the brain to resemble it."

## The "thoughts are thinkers" bridge

Benjamin L's specific argument: in Barrett's view, "thoughts are thinkers" is **quite literally** true. Thoughts (competing neural patterns) think (compete) about what is going on. They interact with each other to determine which thoughts are doing the best job in a given situation. The winner becomes the actual experience.

This is the most concrete proposed mechanism in the wiki for Levin's strongest claim. If Barrett is right, then a thought is **constituted by** a competition that shapes the brain to instantiate it. The thought and the thinking process are not separable.

## Engineering analogue

The connection to AI architecture is striking. Modern transformer decoding works similarly:

- Many candidate continuations exist (top-k sampling).
- They compete via probability.
- The sampled continuation shapes the future context.

This is structurally similar to Barrett's competition among concepts. The difference: in Barrett's view, the **competition is the cognition**, not just the output step. The implication for AI memory is that the right architecture may not be "retrieve then reason" but "let candidate engrams compete to shape the reasoning step."

## Why this matters

[constructed-emotion-barrett](/constructed-emotion-barrett) supplies a mechanism that bridges:

- Damasio's [somatic-marker-hypothesis](/somatic-marker-hypothesis) (emotions as cognitive infrastructure) at the function level.
- Levin's [memories-as-agents](/memories-as-agents) (engrams with minimal agency) at the structural level.
- Conway's [working-self](/working-self) (active goal-hierarchy modulating retrieval) at the cognitive-psychology level.

All three describe the same underlying architecture from different angles.
