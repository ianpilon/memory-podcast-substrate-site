# Autobiographical Knowledge Base

**Summary**: The long-term, hierarchically organized store of self-relevant knowledge. Ranges from the abstract life story at the top, through themes and lifetime periods, to general events and episodic memories at the base.

**Sources**: Conway_2005.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-27

---

## The hierarchy

Conway lays out a partonomic (part-of) hierarchy with several levels (source: Conway_2005.pdf):

- **Life story**: most abstract level, containing general factual and evaluative knowledge about the individual. It may also contain self-images that divide the self into different selves.
- **Themes**: long-running tracks like "work" or "relationships."
- **Lifetime periods**: extended phases such as "Working at University X" or "Friends with Y," each containing knowledge about goals, others, locations, activities, projects, and evaluations common to that period.
- **General events**: repeated or categoric events ("buying a house," "Christmas shopping," "learning to drive"), and mini-histories made of related sub-events.
- **Specific episodic memories**: the experience-near images at the base of the hierarchy. See [episodic-memory](/episodic-memory).

A "specific autobiographical memory" is the conjunction of autobiographical knowledge with one or more episodic memories.

## The general-event basic level

Conway proposes that general events are the preferred level of access, analogous to Rosch's basic level of categorization. They are optimized for informativeness and ease of access. From a general event, both upward (lifetime periods, themes) and downward (specific episodes) traversal is efficient.

## Where the conceptual self lives

The hierarchy connects to the conceptual-self component of the [working-self](/working-self). Self-images at the conceptual-self level have cues that channel access to specific lifetime periods and general events. For example, "I am a researcher" gives differential access to memories from one's professional life.

## What patients with damage show

- **Retrograde amnesia**: often preserves conceptual autobiographical knowledge while losing episodic detail. Some patients with no episodic memories at all still know their past as "stories about themselves."
- **Semantic dementia**: the reverse pattern, with episodic memories surviving while conceptual knowledge degrades.
- **Frontal damage with confabulation**: produces false memories that fit current self-images (positive in some, paranoid in others). See [ptsd-and-memory-distortion](/ptsd-and-memory-distortion).

These dissociations support the SMS view that conceptual autobiographical knowledge and episodic memory are separable systems that normally interlock to form a memory.
