# Human Factors Engineering (HFE)

**Summary**: The discipline of designing technology that fits humans in their actual operating contexts. The Schleisman article presents five HFE principles using Google Glass and the Army IVAS headset as case studies, plus Human Readiness Levels (HRLs) as a maturity scale.

**Sources**: Is-your-technology-human-ready.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-27

## The case studies

### Google Glass

Released 2014. Withdrawn from individual sale 2015. The technical achievement was impressive; the human failure was social. Wearers were rejected ("Glassholes"), privacy concerns mounted, and the device became "a class divide on your face." Google had solved a problem (hunched-over-phones) that turned out not to be the highest-priority user problem, and the solution introduced larger ones (source: Is-your-technology-human-ready.pdf).

### Integrated Visual Augmentation System (IVAS)

Army mixed-reality headset. Originally scheduled for 2021 operation. Pushed to 2025 after testing identified head pain, neck pain, face pressure, nausea, eye strain, and incidental light emission that posed safety threats. In Ops Demo testing, soldiers performed **worse** with the headset than without it. The Army's response was sober: integrate the findings back into design via a "soldier-centered design" process.

## Five HFE principles

1. **Obtain a deep understanding of users' problems before settling on a solution.**
2. **Clearly define user stories and use cases.**
3. **Consider the system in its operational context, not just on the bench.**
4. **Plan for multiple iterations of testing and feedback.** Waterfall is unrealistic.
5. **Establish performance metrics that include operational capabilities**, comparing performance with and without the technology.

These principles are obvious-sounding but hard to apply. The article frames HFE as a discipline that turns the obvious into actionable practice.

## Human Readiness Levels

Defined in ANSI/HFES standard 400-2021 as a counterpart to NASA's Technology Readiness Levels (TRLs). HRLs evaluate how mature a system is with respect to readiness for human use. The 2024 NDIA Human Systems conference and DoD Directive 5000.95 are pushing HRL adoption in defense acquisition.

The point: technical maturity and human usability are separate axes. A TRL-9 system (fully proven technology) at HRL-3 (early human evaluation) is not ready to deploy.

## Connection to the rest of the wiki

The lightest connection in the wiki, but not zero:

- If [ai-memory-as-identity-construction](/ai-memory-as-identity-construction) produces agents with persistent relational state, the **social impact** of those agents on users is an HFE question. Google Glass failed because users could not socially navigate around it. An agent that "remembers everything about you" may fail for similar reasons.
- Conway's framework predicts that agents which form coherent narratives of users will activate users' [working-self](/working-self) in ways that cannot be predicted from technical testing alone. Field deployment matters.
- The IVAS lesson (compare with and without the technology) is the right evaluation strategy for memory-augmented agents too. Does the user perform tasks better with the memory-rich agent than with a stateless one?
