## What the article argues

Two case studies anchor the argument (source: Is-your-technology-human-ready.pdf):

- **Google Glass** (2014): a technically impressive wearable that failed because the social impact and privacy implications were underestimated. Wearers were nicknamed "Glassholes" and Glass was called "a class divide on your face." Glass was withdrawn from individual sale within a year.
- **IVAS** (Army Integrated Visual Augmentation System): a mixed-reality soldier headset that has been pushed from 2021 to 2025 because of head pain, neck pain, face pressure, nausea, eye strain, and incidental light emission. In field tests, soldiers performed missions better without the headset than with it.

## Five HFE principles

1. Obtain a deep understanding of users' problems before settling on a solution.
2. Clearly define user stories and use cases.
3. Consider the system in its operational context, not just on the bench.
4. Plan for multiple iterations of testing and feedback. Waterfall is unrealistic.
5. Establish performance metrics that include operational capabilities, comparing performance with and without the technology.

## Human Readiness Levels

The article introduces HRLs, defined in ANSI/HFES standard 400-2021, as a parallel to NASA's Technology Readiness Levels. The point: technical maturity and human usability are separate axes and both must mature before deployment. The 2024 NDIA Human Systems conference and DoD Directive 5000.95 are cited as evidence that defense acquisition is moving toward HFE as a "have to have."

## Why this might still matter

If [memory-as-substrate-thesis](/memory-as-substrate-thesis) is right and we build AI agents on memory-as-identity architectures, the resulting agents will have personality, narrative, and relational state. The Google Glass failure mode (social rejection of an unfamiliar object on someone's face) has a clear analogue: social rejection of agents that act like they remember you in unfamiliar ways. The HFE discipline is the closest existing playbook for that deployment challenge.
